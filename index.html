I am a fourth-year Biomedicine student at UVic-UCC, currently working at the Computational Biochemistry and Biophysics Lab on the modelling and reverse engineering of biological regulatory systems — trying to understand how networks of interacting components give rise to ordered, often oscillatory behaviour, and how that structure can be recovered from incomplete observations.

My research interests lie at the intersection of dynamical systems, network science, and complex systems. I work on the modelling and analysis of biological networks, combining mechanistic modelling with data-driven methods for inference, prediction, and control. My aim is to build rigorous quantitative frameworks that connect mathematical structure with biological mechanism.

I came to biology through a fascination with disease — the intricate molecular machinery underlying how organisms break down and defend themselves. That led me first to Biochemistry at the Autonomous University of Barcelona, then to a BSc in Biomedicine at the University of Vic – Central University of Catalonia. But something else had always been pulling from underneath: an attraction to abstraction, pattern, and structure. My final pre-university project was on fractals and mathematical pattern formation in natural systems — an early sign, in retrospect, of where I was headed. The tension between these two sides began to resolve during my Erasmus year at the Università degli Studi di Milano, where courses in mathematical modelling for biology, biophysics, and the mathematics behind modern AI made the direction unmistakable. I came back to UVic-UCC and began building that synthesis in practice.
